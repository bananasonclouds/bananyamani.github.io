Characterizing the Next Generation of Robotic Positioners

Universität Zürich Physik-Institut, Supervised by Dr. Marcelle Soares-Santos and Dr. Felipe Andrade Oliveira (in collaboration with EPFL)

Spectroscopic surveys map the large-scale structure of the universe by measuring galaxy redshifts, relying on thousands of robotic fiber positioners to place fibers with micrometer precision. In this work, we present a calibration framework for next-gen positioners, evaluating mechanical and optical performance through tests like XY scanning, tilt response, and lifetime cycling. Our method models kinematics, extracts key metrics like accuracy and repeatability, and helps identify design issues such as backlash or misalignment. This enables reliable, closed-loop control essential for future cosmological instruments.

Star formation in the Orion Nebula

Durham University Physics Department, Supervised by Dr. Russell Smith and Dr. Simon Morris

The Trapezium Cluster is a densely packed region of newly formed stars in the Orion Nebula, where the star formation rate can be calculated using the hydrogen column density to estimate the amount of material available for star formation.

Modelling the Orbits of Main Belt Asteroids

Durham University Physics Department, Supervised by Dr. Mark Swinbank and Dr. Alistair Edge

Created a Python program to predict the future motion of asteroids and plot their 3D orbits by utilizing their right ascension (RA) and declination (Dec) and applying orbital mechanics equations.

International Institute of Astronautical Sciences

Melbourne, Florida, USA : Astronautics 101 Certification (April 2025)

As part of the Astronautics 101 program with the IIAS, I completed training designed to prepare aspiring researchers and communicators for human spaceflight environments. The course combined theory, mission simulation, and physical training to develop real-world astronautical skills.

  • Hypoxia awareness and mitigation
  • Spacesuit operations: donning, doffing, and pressure regulation
  • High-G and microgravity physiology flight (aerobatic aircraft)
  • Mission simulation and Crew Resource Management (CRM) training

Academic Modules Included:

  • The Mesosphere and Lower Thermosphere environment
  • Fundamentals of remote sensing and aerospace cinematography
  • Spaceflight simulation and operations
  • Effective communication and CRM techniques for crewed missions

About Me

I’m Banan Yamani, a physics master’s student at the Physik-Institut der Universität Zürich. My research interests lie at the intersection of instrumentation and cosmology—especially large-scale structure, CMB B-modes, and gravitational waves. I'm particularly passionate about building tools that enable more precise galaxy surveys and deepen our understanding of the universe.

Outside the lab, I’m deeply committed to science communication. I host a bilingual podcast called Spacebites with Benny, where I explain astrophysics concepts in Arabic and English—making the cosmos more accessible across cultures. I also interview people from all backgrounds on cosmology, particle physics, and even astronautics.

My goal is to become an astronaut and conduct research on the International Space Station.
